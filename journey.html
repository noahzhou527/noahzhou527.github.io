Current-State Journey

Climber journey map

Understanding how a climber moves before, during, and between sessions reveals where motivation fades, where progress becomes invisible, and where community connections break down.

Scenario

Climber

A climber wants to keep improving, stay motivated, discover climbing opportunities, and feel connected to a climbing community.

A visual overview of the six stages that shape motivation, progress, and community connection across the climbing journey.

1Planning to Climb 2Getting to the Gym 3Climbing Session 4After the Session 5Between Sessions 6Rejoining Community
1

Stage

Planning to Climb

User Actions

  • Thinks about whether to climb this week
  • Checks messages or social media

Touchpoints

  • Group chats
  • Gym page
  • Friends
  • Memory

Thoughts

"Do I have anyone to go with?"

"Is there anything happening?"

Emotions

Curious, hesitant

Pain Points

  • Hard to know who is available
  • Poor visibility of events or suitable sessions

Opportunities

  • Session discovery
  • Partner-finding
  • Event recommendations
2

Stage

Getting to the Gym

User Actions

  • Arrives at gym
  • Decides what to work on

Touchpoints

  • Gym wall
  • Friends and staff
  • Phone notes or photos

Thoughts

"What should I try today?"

"What did I struggle with last time?"

Emotions

Motivated but uncertain

Pain Points

  • No clear recall of previous routes, projects, or goals

Opportunities

  • Quick pre-session summary
  • Saved projects
  • Suggested goals
3

Stage

Climbing Session

User Actions

  • Attempts routes
  • Asks for help and observes others
  • Takes occasional photos

Touchpoints

  • Physical wall
  • Peers and coach
  • Phone camera

Thoughts

"Am I improving?"

"Should I keep trying this?"

Emotions

Engaged, challenged, sometimes frustrated

Pain Points

  • Improvement feels invisible in the moment
  • Asking others can feel awkward

Opportunities

  • Lightweight logging
  • Route notes
  • Optional beta-sharing
4

Stage

After the Session

User Actions

  • Leaves gym
  • Maybe posts a photo or message
  • Rarely logs systematically

Touchpoints

  • Social media
  • Notes app
  • Memory

Thoughts

"That was fun, but I'll probably forget what I did."

Emotions

Satisfied but inconsistent

Pain Points

  • Session data is lost
  • Achievements are not captured meaningfully

Opportunities

  • One-tap post-session logging
  • Milestone feedback
  • Recap cards
5

Stage

Between Sessions

Key drop-off point

User Actions

  • Tries to stay motivated
  • May not think much about climbing until next time

Touchpoints

  • Social feeds
  • Chat groups
  • Generic fitness apps

Thoughts

"I want to keep going, but it's easy to lose momentum."

Emotions

Motivated, then drifting

Pain Points

  • Motivation drops between sessions
  • No continuity

Opportunities

  • Streaks
  • Micro-goals
  • Encouraging reminders
  • Small challenges
6

Stage

Rejoining Community

User Actions

  • Looks for next session, events, or people to climb with

Touchpoints

  • Gym announcements
  • Friends
  • Community channels

Thoughts

"Who can I climb with next?"

"Is there something beginner-friendly?"

Emotions

Hopeful, sometimes excluded

Pain Points

  • Social connection depends on existing network
  • Opportunities are fragmented

Opportunities

  • Community hub
  • Beginner tags
  • Friend circles
  • Small-group challenges
Key Insights

The biggest breakdowns happen after the climb, not just during it

Progress invisibility

Improvement is hard to see in the moment and easy to forget afterward, so growth does not feel reinforced.

Fragmented social connection

Finding partners, events, and beginner-friendly opportunities depends too much on existing networks.

Motivation drops between sessions

The sharpest break in continuity happens once the session ends, when momentum fades and no clear loop brings the user back.

Users may enjoy climbing, yet still struggle to sustain participation because progress is hard to see, social opportunities are hard to discover, and motivation fades once the session ends.

Reading The Map

The most important themes revealed here are motivation, progress visibility, and social connectedness. A successful system should not simply digitise logging; it should connect these moments into a more continuous experience.

Journey-to-Design Link

From analysis to requirements

The journey map directly informs the next-stage requirements. Pain points around fragmented planning suggest the need for partner-finding and event discovery. Pain points during and after climbing suggest the need for low-friction logging and clear progress summaries. Pain points between sessions suggest the need for playful motivational loops, such as milestones, streaks, or friendly challenges. Together, these opportunities create a strong bridge from journey analysis to concrete user, functional, and playful requirements.
